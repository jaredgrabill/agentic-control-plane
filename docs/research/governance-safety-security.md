# Research Brief: Governance, Safety, and Security of Enterprise Agentic AI Platforms

> Compiled 2026-07-10 as input to the platform architecture. All claims were
> verified via web search/fetch in July 2026 unless flagged "from training
> data." See [README](README.md) for the research index.

## 1. Security frameworks

**OWASP GenAI Security Project — LLM Top 10 (2025).** The current edition of the [OWASP Top 10 for LLM Applications 2025](https://genai.owasp.org/resource/owasp-top-10-for-llm-applications-2025/) lists: LLM01 Prompt Injection, LLM02 Sensitive Information Disclosure, LLM03 Supply Chain, LLM04 Data and Model Poisoning, LLM05 Improper Output Handling, LLM06 Excessive Agency, LLM07 System Prompt Leakage, LLM08 Vector and Embedding Weaknesses, LLM09 Misinformation, LLM10 Unbounded Consumption ([official list](https://genai.owasp.org/llm-top-10/)). For agent platforms, LLM01, LLM05, LLM06 (excessive agency), and LLM08 are the load-bearing entries.

**OWASP Agentic Security Initiative (ASI).** Two key documents:

- *Agentic AI — Threats and Mitigations* (Feb 2025): a threat-model-based taxonomy including T1 Memory Poisoning, T2 Tool Misuse, T3 Privilege Compromise, plus resource overload, cascading hallucination, intent manipulation, identity spoofing, human-in-the-loop overwhelming ("HITL fatigue"), and rogue agents ([OWASP resource page](https://genai.owasp.org/resource/agentic-ai-threats-and-mitigations/)). Prescribed mitigations: memory content validation and session isolation (T1); tool access verification, usage-pattern monitoring, and full execution logging (T2); strict permission scoping and audit of role inheritance (T3).
- *OWASP Top 10 for Agentic Applications 2026*, released **December 9, 2025** at Black Hat Europe, peer-reviewed by 100+ experts ([announcement](https://genai.owasp.org/resource/owasp-top-10-for-agentic-applications/)): **ASI01** Agent Goal Hijack, **ASI02** Tool Misuse and Exploitation, **ASI03** Identity and Privilege Abuse, **ASI04** Agentic Supply Chain Vulnerabilities, **ASI05** Unexpected Code Execution, **ASI06** Memory and Context Poisoning, **ASI07** Insecure Inter-Agent Communication, **ASI08** Cascading Failures, **ASI09** Human-Agent Trust Exploitation, **ASI10** Rogue Agents ([Promptfoo summary](https://www.promptfoo.dev/docs/red-team/owasp-agentic-ai/), [Palo Alto analysis](https://www.paloaltonetworks.com/blog/cloud-security/owasp-agentic-ai-security/)). OWASP has since published a *State of Agentic AI Security and Governance 2.0* and an [AIUC-1 crosswalk](https://genai.owasp.org/) (May 2026) mapping the agentic Top 10 to insurability/assurance requirements. The supply-chain risk (ASI04) is no longer theoretical: the ClawHub skill registry for the OpenClaw open-source agent was systematically poisoned with malware in Q1 2026 (reported by multiple secondary sources, e.g. [MATRA case study](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.10763); exact scale figures unverified).

**MITRE ATLAS.** [ATLAS](https://atlas.mitre.org/) is the adversarial-ML analogue of ATT&CK. In **October 2025 MITRE integrated 14 new agent-focused techniques/sub-techniques contributed by Zenity Labs**, e.g., AI Agent Context Poisoning, Memory Manipulation, AI Agent Tool Credential Harvesting, and "AI Agent Clickbait" (adversarial content crafted to steer agent decisions) ([Zenity](https://zenity.io/blog/current-events/mitre-atlas-ai-security), [NHI Mgmt Group](https://nhimg.org/articles/mitre-atlas-and-agentic-ai-security-what-practitioners-need-to-know/)). Secondary sources put the framework at roughly 16 tactics / 84 techniques / 42 case studies after the late-2025 updates ([Vectra](https://www.vectra.ai/topics/mitre-atlas)); exact current version numbers could not be verified on atlas.mitre.org directly. The practitioner takeaway that *is* consistent across sources: ATLAS's 2025–26 direction shifts threat modeling from model-centric attacks to the execution layer — workflow chaining, delegated-authority persistence, and orchestration/API abuse.

**NIST AI RMF + Generative AI Profile.** The [AI RMF 1.0](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework) (Govern/Map/Measure/Manage) remains the umbrella; the [Generative AI Profile, NIST AI 600-1](https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/ai/NIST.AI.600-1.pdf) (July 2024) adds 12 GenAI risk categories and ~200 suggested actions. Newer developments: NIST released a preliminary draft **Cyber AI Profile (NIST IR 8596)** in December 2025 bridging the AI RMF and CSF 2.0, and in 2026 launched an **AI agent standards effort expected to yield voluntary guidelines on agent identity/authorization, security, and monitoring/logging** ([NIST](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework), secondary confirmation via [Glacis guide](https://www.glacis.io/guide-nist-ai-rmf)). The Cloud Security Alliance has published a community **[Agentic NIST AI RMF Profile](https://labs.cloudsecurityalliance.org/agentic/agentic-nist-ai-rmf-profile-v1/)** mapping RMF functions onto agent-specific controls. CSA's MAESTRO threat-modeling framework for agentic systems (7-layer model, Feb 2025) is also widely referenced (from training data, consistent with [secondary sources](https://techjacksolutions.com/ai/agentic-ai/secure/agent-threat-landscape/)).

## 2. Regulatory context

**EU AI Act — critical mid-2026 update.** The original schedule (prohibitions Feb 2025; GPAI-provider obligations Aug 2025; most remaining obligations Aug 2, 2026) has been **materially amended by the "Digital Omnibus on AI."** The Commission proposed the omnibus on Nov 19, 2025; political agreement was reached **May 6–7, 2026**, Parliament endorsed it June 16, and the Council gave final approval **June 29, 2026** — entry into force is imminent as of this writing ([Consilium press release](https://www.consilium.europa.eu/en/press/press-releases/2026/05/07/artificial-intelligence-council-and-parliament-agree-to-simplify-and-streamline-rules/), [Gibson Dunn](https://www.gibsondunn.com/eu-ai-act-omnibus-agreement-postponed-high-risk-deadlines-and-other-key-changes/), [White & Case](https://www.whitecase.com/insight-alert/eu-agrees-digital-omnibus-deal-simplify-ai-rules)). Net effect for enterprise deployers:

- **High-risk (Annex III) obligations deferred from Aug 2, 2026 to Dec 2, 2027**; Annex I embedded-product systems to **Aug 2, 2028** ([DLA Piper](https://knowledge.dlapiper.com/dlapiperknowledge/globalemploymentlatestdevelopments/2026/The-Digital-AI-Omnibus-Proposed-deferral-of-high-risk-AI-obligations-under-the-AI-Act)).
- **Article 50 transparency duties** (disclosing AI interaction, marking synthetic content) and **Commission enforcement powers over GPAI providers (with fines) still take effect Aug 2, 2026** ([EU AI Act implementation timeline](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/implementation-timeline/), [AI Act Service Desk](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/timeline/timeline-implementation-eu-ai-act)).
- GPAI provider obligations (Art. 53/55, model documentation, systemic-risk duties) have applied since Aug 2, 2025; deployers embedding GPAI in workflows inherit obligations mainly via Art. 4 (AI literacy, in force since Feb 2025), Art. 50 transparency, and Art. 26 deployer duties (human oversight, input data control, logging retention) once high-risk classification applies. Agentic HR, credit, or access-control workflows are prime Annex III candidates — the deferral buys time but conformity work should proceed now.

**ISO/IEC 42001:2023** ([ISO](https://www.iso.org/standard/42001)) specifies a certifiable **AI Management System (AIMS)** — risk/impact assessment, AI policy, lifecycle controls, human oversight, transparency — analogous to ISO 27001 for infosec. It is emerging as the primary third-party certification signal for AI platform vendors; Microsoft, AWS, and others hold certifications ([Microsoft compliance page](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/compliance/regulatory/offering-iso-42001)). Auditor guidance emphasizes it complements, not replaces, 27001 ([Schellman](https://www.schellman.com/blog/iso-certifications/iso-42001-lessons-learned), [CSA](https://cloudsecurityalliance.org/blog/2025/05/08/iso-42001-lessons-learned-from-auditing-and-implementing-the-framework)).

**SOC 2.** SOC 2 remains an attestation against the Trust Services Criteria with **no AI-specific criteria**; practice in 2025–26 is to fold AI controls into existing criteria (change management for model/prompt versions, logical access for agent identities, processing integrity for agent outputs) or add ISO 42001/NIST AI RMF mappings as a "SOC 2+" examination ([Schellman on AI in SOC 2](https://www.schellman.com/blog/soc-examinations/how-to-incorporate-ai-into-your-soc-2-examination), [comparison](https://www.barradvisory.com/resource/soc-iso-ai-compliance/)). Buyers increasingly ask for SOC 2 (platform security) *plus* ISO 42001 (AI governance).

## 3. Identity & auth for agents

Consensus 2026 architecture treats every agent instance as a **non-human identity (NHI)** with its own credential, never a shared API key:

- **Workload identity via SPIFFE/SPIRE**: each agent workload is attested at runtime and issued short-lived X.509/JWT **SVIDs** for mTLS service identity ([spiffe.io](https://spiffe.io/docs/latest/deploying/spire_agent/), [Red Hat: SPIFFE + token exchange + Kagenti for zero-trust agent identity](https://next.redhat.com/2026/06/10/wiring-zero-trust-identity-for-ai-agents-spiffe-token-exchange-and-kagenti/)).
- **Stateless JWT + RBAC** for request-path authorization, with **short-lived, audience-bound tokens** (minutes, not days) so a hijacked agent's credential expires quickly; roles map to agent archetypes, scopes map to individual tools.
- **RFC 8693 OAuth Token Exchange** as the workhorse for crossing trust boundaries: the agent exchanges its identity token for a narrower, downstream-audience token carrying the user's delegation context, preserving the chain in `act`/`may_act` claims ([RFC 8693](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc8693), [SPIFFE vs OAuth for NHIs](https://aembit.io/blog/spiffe-vs-oauth-access-control-nonhuman-identities/)).
- **On-behalf-of delegation for agents is being standardized**: the IETF draft [*OAuth 2.0 Extension: On-Behalf-Of User Authorization for AI Agents*](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/draft-oauth-ai-agents-on-behalf-of-user/01/) adds `requested_actor` and `actor_token` parameters so the specific agent's identity flows through user consent into the access token, and the full user→client→agent delegation chain is auditable in token claims. Related drafts: [draft-klrc-aiagent-auth](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/draft-klrc-aiagent-auth/) and [draft-mcguinness-oauth-actor-profile](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/draft-mcguinness-oauth-actor-profile-00).
- **MCP authorization**: since the Nov 2025 spec revision, internet-exposed MCP servers must implement **OAuth 2.1 with PKCE**, acting as OAuth resource servers with RFC 9728 Protected Resource Metadata; the [2026-07-28 release candidate](https://blog.modelcontextprotocol.io/posts/2026-07-28-release-candidate/) tightens alignment with OAuth/OIDC and requires that server-initiated elicitation only occur during active client requests ([MCP authorization spec](https://modelcontextprotocol.io/specification/draft/basic/authorization)).
- **Least privilege / per-tool authorization**: grant scopes per tool (not per agent), use just-in-time credential issuance, and separate the agent's *identity* from the *permissions of the human it serves* — an agent should hold the intersection, never the union ([Indigo Consulting blueprint](https://www.indigoconsulting.ca/blog/the-enterprise-blueprint-for-agentic-identity-workload-attestation-and-lifecycle-governance/)). This directly mitigates OWASP ASI03 and ATLAS credential-harvesting techniques.

## 4. Policy enforcement

The dominant pattern is an **externalized Policy Decision Point (PDP) in front of every tool call** — authorization the model cannot talk its way around:

- **Engines**: [OPA/Rego](https://codilime.com/blog/why-use-open-policy-agent-for-your-ai-agents/) (CNCF-graduated, general-purpose), [Cedar](https://www.cedarpolicy.com/) (AWS-built, now CNCF, formally analyzable), and Casbin (lightweight embedded RBAC/ABAC — from training data, unchanged). Comparative guidance: Cedar for formally verifiable, default-deny tool authorization; OPA for heterogeneous infrastructure policy ([OPA vs Cedar for MCP](https://natoma.ai/blog/mcp-access-control-opa-vs-cedar-the-definitive-guide), [policy-as-code for agent loops](https://tianpan.co/blog/2026-04-25-policy-as-code-agent-permissions-opa-rego)).
- **Managed enforcement**: **Policy in Amazon Bedrock AgentCore** uses Cedar at the AgentCore Gateway between agent and tools — default-deny, forbid-overrides-permit, with natural-language-to-Cedar authoring validated by automated reasoning ([AWS blog](https://aws.amazon.com/blogs/security/why-policy-in-amazon-bedrock-agentcore-chose-cedar-for-securing-agentic-workflows/), [docs](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/bedrock-agentcore/latest/devguide/policy.html)). This gateway-PDP pattern is the reference architecture regardless of vendor.
- **Action-level guardrails**: three-way decisions — allow / deny / **require-approval** — are now first-class in agent SDKs (e.g., [Vercel AI SDK policy-based tool approvals](https://ai-sdk.dev/docs/agents/policy-tool-approvals), Claude Agent SDK permission modes) and in Microsoft's [Agent Governance Toolkit](https://microsoft.github.io/agent-governance-toolkit/tutorials/08-opa-rego-cedar-policies/).
- **Human-in-the-loop gates** for irreversible or high-blast-radius actions (payments, deletions, external communications, privilege changes), with the caveat that OWASP explicitly identifies *HITL overwhelming/approval fatigue* as a threat — approvals must be rationed to genuinely consequential actions.
- **Blast-radius controls**: budget caps, rate limits, per-session spend/action quotas, environment isolation (staging-first execution), and reversibility requirements (dry-run, soft-delete) ([Atlan guardrails checklist](https://atlan.com/know/ai-agent/enterprise-ai-agent-guardrails-checklist/)).
- **Kill switches**: enterprise guidance converges on tiered deactivation — pause one agent, suspend one channel/tool, or halt the fleet — each with a named owner, tested runbook, and activation without vendor involvement; EU AI Act Art. 14 human-oversight requirements for high-risk systems effectively mandate an interrupt capability (["stop button"](https://www.openlegion.ai/en/learn/ai-agent-governance), [governance checklist](https://jservo.com/govern-ai-agents-enterprise-2026-framework/)).

## 5. Content & behavior guardrails

- **Prompt injection defenses**: no complete fix exists; state of the art is architectural. The six **design patterns** from Beurer-Kellner et al. (action-selector, plan-then-execute, LLM-map-reduce, dual-LLM, code-then-execute, context-minimization) remain the canonical reference ([Simon Willison's summary](https://simonwillison.net/2025/Jun/13/prompt-injection-design-patterns/)). Google DeepMind's **CaMeL** operationalizes the dual-LLM pattern: a privileged LLM writes a plan in a sandboxed DSL, a quarantined LLM handles untrusted data, and capability-based data-flow tracking blocks tainted values from reaching sensitive tools; 2025–26 work extends it to enterprise and computer-use settings ([CaMeL analysis](https://afine.com/llm-security-prompt-injection-camel), [arXiv extensions](https://arxiv.org/html/2505.22852v1)).
- **Input/output filtering & PII redaction**: layered classifier stacks — e.g., **NeMo Guardrails** orchestrating **Llama Guard 3** (hazard taxonomy) plus a fast prompt-injection classifier, with **Microsoft Presidio** or GLiNER-PII for PII detection/redaction on inputs and outputs ([NeMo Guardrails](https://github.com/NVIDIA-NeMo/Guardrails), [production reference](https://www.digitalapplied.com/blog/llm-guardrails-production-safety-layers-reference-2026)). Managed equivalents: Azure AI Content Safety, AWS Bedrock Guardrails (latter from training data).
- **System-prompt/constitutional hardening**: explicit behavioral constraints, instruction-hierarchy training, and spotlighting/delimiting of untrusted content help but are bypassable — treat as one layer, never the control (consistent across [OWASP LLM01 guidance](https://genai.owasp.org/llm-top-10/) and academic surveys such as [this 2026 attack/defense survey](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2603.11088)).
- **Sandboxing tool execution**: for agent-generated code, the 2026 consensus is **microVMs (Firecracker, Kata) or gVisor**, not plain containers — kernel-shared containers are considered insufficient for untrusted LLM-generated code; Firecracker cold starts of ~90–200 ms make per-task VMs practical, and a market of dedicated agent-sandbox platforms (E2B, Daytona) has formed ([Northflank guide](https://northflank.com/blog/how-to-sandbox-ai-agents), [E2B/Firecracker setup](https://www.spheron.network/blog/ai-agent-code-execution-sandbox-e2b-daytona-firecracker/)). Network egress allowlisting from the sandbox is equally important to contain exfiltration (widely recommended; also a CaMeL-adjacent control).

## 6. Audit

Requirements converging across EU AI Act Art. 12/26 (logging & retention for high-risk systems), ISO 42001, and SOC 2 evidence needs:

- **Immutable, append-only audit trails**: every agent step — prompt, model+version, retrieved context, guardrail verdicts, policy decisions (which policy allowed/denied), tool calls with parameters and results, human approvals/overrides, kill-switch activations — written to append-only storage with timestamps and actor identity ([OpenLegion governance guide](https://www.openlegion.ai/en/learn/ai-agent-governance), [Atlan checklist](https://atlan.com/know/ai-agent/enterprise-ai-agent-guardrails-checklist/)). WORM/object-lock storage and hash-chaining are common implementations (from training data).
- **Decision provenance**: records must tie each action to the delegation chain (user → client → agent, via OAuth `act`/`obo` claims) and to the exact model version, policy set version, and knowledge snapshot in force — this is where identity (§3) and policy (§4) plumbing pays off in audit ([WorkOS on OBO auditability](https://workos.com/blog/oauth-on-behalf-of-ai-agents)).
- **Replayability**: the ability to reconstruct an interaction step-by-step with the artifacts in force at the time is emerging as a de facto examiner expectation in regulated industries ([Lorikeet on replayable audit trails](https://www.lorikeetcx.ai/articles/best-ai-agents-full-audit-trails-regulated-2026)). Retention guidance varies; EU AI Act requires high-risk log retention of at least six months (Art. 19), while some compliance regimes push 3+ years (secondary sources; verify per-jurisdiction).

## Flagged as unverified / low-confidence

MITRE ATLAS exact current version and technique counts; the "~90% incident reduction from sandboxing" statistic (vendor research); exact scale of the ClawHub poisoning incident; 36-month log-retention "requirement" (appears to be consultant guidance, not statute). Casbin details, MAESTRO, AWS Bedrock Guardrails, and WORM-storage implementation patterns are from training data but low-risk.

## Bottom line for a platform builder in mid-2026

Adopt OWASP ASI Top 10 + MITRE ATLAS as the threat model; NIST AI RMF/GenAI Profile + ISO 42001 as the governance scaffold; SPIFFE-attested per-agent identity with RFC 8693 token exchange and short-lived scoped JWTs; a default-deny Cedar/OPA PDP on every tool call with tiered allow/deny/approve and a tested kill switch; layered guardrails plus microVM sandboxing for execution; and append-only, provenance-rich, replayable audit logs. The EU AI Act's high-risk deadline just moved to Dec 2027, but transparency (Art. 50) and GPAI enforcement land Aug 2, 2026.
